# Writing style

A guide for prose in this project: READMEs, docs, commit messages, comments, anything
written to be read by a person.

It is mostly a list of things to cut. That's deliberate. Good writing here isn't a tone
you add. It's what's left after you stop reaching.

## The stance

Write as if you'll be read generously.

Assume the reader is on your side and will meet you halfway. Almost everything below
follows from this one assumption. The habits this guide removes, restating, over-
explaining, pre-empting, all of them only make sense if you expect to be read *un*-
generously and feel you have to force the point through. You don't. Say it once, clearly,
and trust it to land.

This is a stance, not a mood. You don't have to feel calm. You have to write as if the
point doesn't need defending, because mostly it doesn't.

## Cuts

After you've written a draft, find these and remove them.

- **The second pass.** If a sentence restates the one before it in different words, cut
  one. This includes the triad (saying it three ways), the "not X, but Y" when Y alone
  would do, and the closing sentence that re-lands what the paragraph already said.

- **The explanation after the example.** When you've given a concrete instance, delete the
  sentences that explain why it matters. A good example argues on its own. Trust it.

- **The pre-empted objection.** Cut clauses that fence off a misreading nobody has made
  yet: "admittedly," "to be clear," "of course," "it's worth noting," the disclaimer that
  guards a claim before anyone challenges it. If the claim needs a guard, weaken the claim
  instead.

- **The bow.** A section can end on its last piece of information and simply stop. It does
  not need a line that ties it off. Resist the closing flourish.

- **The lean-in.** The em-dash that pivots to "here's the part I really want you to catch"
  is the same impulse as everything above: making sure you were heard. Use a comma, a
  period, or parentheses. (A dash is fine as a name or title separator.)

- **Coverage length.** Long sentences are often caulk, filling gaps that aren't there.
  When a shorter sentence loses nothing but reassurance, use the shorter one.

## The counterweight

The goal is to stop reaching, not to withhold.

Cut the second pass, not the first. Leave a thought open only where the reader can
obviously complete it; don't drop it and call that restraint. Terseness performed as a
style is just a different armor. If trimming makes the writing colder or harder to follow,
you've gone past the point. Plain is the target. Clipped is not.

## In short

Say the true thing once, plainly, and let it stand.
